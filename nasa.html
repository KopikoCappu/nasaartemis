Mission Overview

Beyond
Earth's
Reach

Artemis II is the first crewed mission of NASA's Artemis program — and the first time humans have ventured beyond low Earth orbit since Apollo 17 in December 1972. The crew of four will fly a free-return trajectory around the Moon, reaching a distance of approximately 4,700 miles beyond the lunar far side before returning home.

This mission doesn't land — but it proves every system needed to eventually put boots on the Moon again and build a permanent lunar presence. Orion, SLS, and human deep-space endurance all get their ultimate test.

Why It Matters

History in
the Making

🌕

50 Years Since Apollo

No human has been to the lunar vicinity since Eugene Cernan stepped off the Moon on December 14, 1972. Artemis II breaks that 54-year silence — with a crew that looks like all of humanity.

🚀

Proving Deep Space Systems

SLS and Orion must prove they can keep humans alive beyond Earth's magnetic protection — enduring radiation, extreme temperatures, and the psychological weight of profound isolation.

🏕

Gateway to a Lunar Base

This is step two of a long march. Artemis IV and V target actual lunar landings by 2028, with a permanent Artemis Base Camp on the Moon's south pole planned for the 2030s.

Mission Timeline

10 Days
Around the Moon

Day 0 — April 1, 2026 · T-0: 6:24 PM EDT

Liftoff from KSC Pad 39B

The Space Launch System ignites its RS-25 engines and solid rocket boosters, generating 8.8 million pounds of thrust. Four astronauts accelerate from zero to orbital velocity in under nine minutes. First crewed SLS launch in history.

Day 1–2 — Translunar Injection

Escaping Earth's Orbit

Orion performs a trans-lunar injection burn, setting the crew on a free-return trajectory toward the Moon. No burn is needed to return — gravity does the work if the engine fails. The crew tests all life support and navigation systems en route.

Day 4–5 — Closest Lunar Approach

Swinging Around the Far Side

Orion loops around the Moon's far side, passing approximately 7,600 km beyond the surface. Communication with Earth goes dark for several minutes. The crew will see Earthrise — the first humans to do so in over five decades.

Day 10 — Splashdown

Pacific Ocean Recovery

Orion reenters Earth's atmosphere at 25,000 mph — faster than any crewed vehicle since Apollo — before parachutes deploy and the capsule splashes down in the Pacific. The crew has proven the path to the Moon is open again.

The Artemis Program

Artemis I → V

The Artemis program is a sustained campaign, not a single sprint. Artemis I proved the hardware. Artemis II proves human survival in deep space. Artemis III tests landers in orbit. Then the Moon gets human footprints again — and eventually, a base.
